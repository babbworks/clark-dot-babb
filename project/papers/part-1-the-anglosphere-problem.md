# Part 1: The Anglosphere Problem

*First in a series on industrial activation and the case for Clark.*

---

We are facing a kaleidoscope of issues and ills across what Neal Stephenson called the Anglosphere in his 1995 science fiction novel *The Diamond Age*. The term has entered the mainstream since then via authors like James C. Bennett and historians like John Lloyd. In the three decades since it was coined, many proposals have been made for Canada and the United States, Australia, New Zealand, and the United Kingdom to work together as an economic bloc. Despite limited traction among policymakers, the concept is not so new. In the 1800s various British thinkers — Charles Dilke in *Greater Britain*, J.R. Seeley in *The Expansion of England* — recognized the emergence of something different from the Empire itself: a metastasizing of the English world into a diverse but common culture across continents, bound together not by imperial decree but by shared legal inheritance, shared language, and, most importantly for our purposes, a shared experience of industrialization.

There is no universal definition for who belongs to this informal family. Ireland, South Africa, some Caribbean states, and even Singapore have been included in it. But regardless of how the borders are drawn, the core members share three things that matter now more than at any point in the postwar period: Common Law frameworks that make contract-heavy, distributed enterprise structurally natural; a language of business and science that travels without translation; and the shared experience of what we must now call early industrialization, as we watch South America, Asia, and Africa fire up their engines and build the productive capacity we once took for granted.

The prototyping of the industrial society happened in these countries and across Europe first, passing through several stages, world wars, and financial restructuring phases, which then led to a marked deindustrialization phase. This brings us to a new moment characterized by the cry for reindustrialization, advanced manufacturing, reshoring, and the sovereign society. But to understand why this moment is so urgent, and why the usual responses are insufficient, we need to look at the numbers — because the decline we are living through is not the gentle evolution that the phrase "post-industrial economy" implies. It is a collapse, and it is peculiar to our family of nations.

## The Divergence

In 1970, manufacturing accounted for roughly 23 per cent of GDP in the United States. Today it sits at about 10 per cent — a 57 per cent relative decline in half a century. The United Kingdom has fallen further, from approximately 25 per cent to 8 per cent, the steepest drop in the developed world. Canada has been cut nearly in half, from about 20 per cent to 10. Australia's decline is the most dramatic of all: from 25 per cent to 5.5 per cent. New Zealand has traced a similar arc, from 22 per cent down to single digits.

These are not universal trends. They did not happen everywhere. Germany started from a comparable position and sits today at roughly 18 per cent. Japan, at about 21 per cent. South Korea actually grew its manufacturing share — from 14 per cent in 1970 to over 25 per cent today, making it the most industrialized OECD country. Robert Atkinson of the Information Technology and Innovation Foundation has documented this divergence systematically, identifying what he calls the "Anglo-Saxon versus Listian" split: the Anglosphere nations average an advanced industries location quotient of 0.81 — below the global average of 1.0 — while their East Asian and German counterparts average 1.45. Labour productivity growth in the Listian economies runs more than four times higher.

The point is not that deindustrialization is unique to us. Every advanced economy has seen manufacturing's share of GDP decline somewhat. The point is that the Anglosphere experienced something categorically worse — a catastrophic hollowing that the comparator nations managed to avoid through institutional choices we did not make. Germany maintained its dual apprenticeship system, its Sparkassen regional banks providing patient capital to small manufacturers, its Fraunhofer network translating research into shop-floor applications, and its IHK chambers ensuring every enterprise is connected to an institutional support network. We dismantled or never built equivalents in each domain. The result is visible in one number: advanced industries location quotient of 0.46 for Australia, 0.58 for Canada, 0.67 for the United Kingdom, 0.87 for the United States. We are below average in the industries of the future, in the countries that invented modern industry.

In 1981, Geoffrey Howe, then Chancellor of the Exchequer, wrote privately to Margaret Thatcher after the Toxteth riots and asked whether Britain should pursue what he called "managed decline" for Liverpool — a frank admission that some places were to be written off. He later denied the phrase, but the cabinet papers released in 2011 confirmed it. What Howe articulated as a radical option for one city became, in effect, the default industrial policy of the entire Anglosphere for the next four decades: not a deliberate plan, but an absence of one, masked by the comforting language of the knowledge economy and the services transition. Computer chips or potato chips — it was all GDP.

We are now living in the consequences of that absence.

## The Pattern That Repeats

At the precise moment that national independence is being emphasized like never before, with supply chain risks and artificial intelligence autonomy acting as driving forces, on what possible grounds can we pursue cooperation among these like-minded countries? And is it possible to do so in ways that provide models and mechanisms for our many trading partners, fostering political stability and material prosperity abroad, ultimately putting less stress on our borders and budgets?

I believe there is a path for impactful cooperation that does not rely on the fickle rhetoric or permissions of our national governments but nonetheless helps each national economy return to and surpass its former strength. But to find it, we must first understand what we actually lost — not the factories themselves, but the ecosystems that made the factories possible.

Every successful industrial ecosystem in history — from Manchester in the 1800s to Shenzhen today — has been sustained by the same three mechanisms, first identified by the economist Alfred Marshall in 1890 and confirmed by every case study since. Marshall called them the three advantages of localization. In plain language: knowledge in the air, a pool of skilled workers, and a network of specialist suppliers.

In Manchester, the Literary and Philosophical Society diffused frontier science into industrial practice, the Mechanics' Institute translated that science into workforce capability, and the cotton trade generated a diversified supplier backbone of chemical works, engineering firms, and foundries. In Birmingham, the Small Master system — hundreds of independent craftspeople each responsible for one step in a process — created what we would now call a distributed production zone, so productive that GDP per capita ran 10 per cent above the national average by the 1960s. In the Connecticut River Valley, the Springfield Armory served as anchor customer, R&D sponsor, and IP diffuser simultaneously, creating a precision tool industry so potent that Massachusetts, Connecticut, and Rhode Island builders collectively shipped 25 per cent of the country's machine tools. In Detroit, the Society of Automotive Engineers published its first standard in 1912; by 1921 those standards were saving the industry an estimated seven million dollars annually, making it possible for hundreds of independent suppliers to produce interchangeable components for multiple assemblers. SAE was, in effect, the invisible infrastructure of an entire ecosystem.

And in Shenzhen, designated China's first Special Economic Zone in 1980 with fewer than 30,000 workers, the Huaqiangbei electronics market — thirty times the size of Tokyo's Akihabara — became a real-time market intelligence system, matchmaking platform, and trust network all at once, while the gongkai model of shared design documentation created the same ambient knowledge transfer that Marshall had described a century earlier on a different continent.

These ecosystems were not designed from a single blueprint. They emerged. But every one of them required institutional support to sustain the conditions for emergence. And here is the fact that matters for everything that follows: in every case where one of these ecosystems died, the cause was the same. Not foreign competition. Not technological change. The cause was that no entity was responsible for maintaining the ecosystem as a whole.

Birmingham's workshop economy was actively dismantled by the Distribution of Industry Act of 1945, which required permits for new factory construction — controls that made it impossible to regenerate businesses or start new ones. Between 1971 and 1987, Birmingham lost almost a third of its total employment, going from one of Britain's most prosperous cities to one of its poorest in a single generation. The Springfield Armory closed in 1968; within two decades, the Connecticut River Valley had gone from America's Silicon Valley to a deindustrialized corridor where skilled machinists could find only service-sector work. Detroit's supplier ecosystem was hollowed not by imports but by the sequential decisions of automakers to internalize and then offshore the supplier relationships that SAE had made possible.

The ecosystem is the thing. Not the factory. Not the strategy document. Not the grant programme. The ecosystem — the web of knowledge, skill, and supplier relationships that lets any one firm operate and every new entrant start. When it is alive, everything accelerates. When it dies, no amount of strategy or subsidy can substitute for it, because what died was not a set of assets but a set of relationships and capabilities that took decades to build and can be lost in years.

## Output-Drag

If we are to succeed beyond our imagination and break through into a new era in which the concept of managed decline is remembered as a shameful stopgap measure, we must turn our attention back to the regional ecosystems of producers and processors of every size, seeing past the poster-boy Shenzhen and back into our own origins, where extreme intra-regional reliance produced the small-scale resilience and momentum that led us into our uncontested positions.

Reshoring is of course not simply bringing large industrial operations back into our countries. It is about simultaneously reviving and unleashing the creative capacities of the ecosystems of small and medium makers and inventors that assist big actors, all the while generating the new approaches and processes that the reinvention of traditional sectors requires. This is a return to the wild west, the wild midwest, and to the mania of Manchester, where rules and reporting took a back seat to the values of rapid adaptation and adoption, to the seizing of momentary opportunities in the supply chain that could produce temporary gains or recurring revenues. It did not matter which; the order of the day was to build the economy and country in one messy, swift, chaotic storm of collective effort.

The gaps between demand and supply that drove those storms were of a specific sort which seems to be misunderstood in our age of services and knowledge work. As the industrial economy advanced, especially in the mid-1800s, the gap between what people intended to produce and what the machinists and fabricators could actually create resulted in differing amounts of what I call output-drag across regions. Wherever the fabrication backbone was the most adaptive and most skilled, all other industries closer to the consumer accelerated, feeding back in on themselves as manufacturing advanced to new levels of capability. The large factory was never the issue. The bottlenecks lay elsewhere — in the supplier of parts and products and processes which enabled the big players to ramp up operations.

Output-drag is the friction between manufacturing intent and fabrication capacity in a given region. Where it is low, everything moves. Where it is high, strategies sit on shelves, lots sit behind fences, and reshoring announcements produce press releases but not production lines. Germany's output-drag is low because four interlocking institutions keep the fabrication backbone alive. The Anglosphere's output-drag is high and rising, because we let those institutions decay or never built them, and no amount of tariff policy or tax incentive can substitute for the missing backbone. A 2025 IndustryWeek survey of more than five hundred manufacturers found that a larger, stronger skilled workforce would bring back 30 per cent of currently offshored production — seven percentage points more than the effect of 15 per cent tariffs across all imports. Workforce beats tariffs. Capability beats incentives. The ecosystem beats everything.

The businesses in need of the most assistance are often too saddled with reporting requirements, thinned-out management, and back orders to engage in strategic planning and relationship building. Instead of asking more from these fragile contributors, the answer is to build what I call an activation protocol — a deliberate, repeatable method for lowering output-drag in a given region by providing the institutional backbone that the Anglosphere's industrial towns no longer have.

## Electronics Is the Oxygen

We have lived through a few rounds of electronics adoption and despite the current frenzy, the next stage is not a cut-and-dry matter of embedding AI. In the workspaces where humans and mechanical systems interact in complex and unique ways, the real challenge is to integrate computers in highly tailored configurations that meet the needs of the human operators. These systems must support role evolution and product-line pivoting without cost-prohibitive retooling of the digital layer. Until now, enterprise software systems often ended up being no different than the dinosaur machines that chain businesses to their past — serving what exists but incapable of meeting the emerging moment.

But the software challenge rests on a more fundamental one that is rarely stated plainly: electronics has become the oxygen of modern industry. It is no longer a sector among sectors. It sits inside every industrial operation and, increasingly, inside every industrial product. The machines that make things run on embedded control; the things they make ship with sensors, firmware, and connectivity. An industrial ecosystem without local electronics capability is breathing through a straw — every product pivot, every process adjustment, every retrofit waits on a distant supplier's queue.

The numbers make the dependency stark. Asia produces more than 85 per cent of global PCB output; North America accounts for roughly 5 per cent. The IPC's April 2024 workforce white paper found that among production jobs in electronics, general assembler and hand solderer are the most difficult roles to fill. The Reshoring Initiative reports that computer and electronics was the leading sector for reshoring in 2024, accounting for 28 per cent of all announced jobs. The CHIPS Act alone faces a workforce gap of 59,000 to 146,000 workers by 2029. Nearly 1.9 million manufacturing jobs may go unfilled by 2033 without intervention.

Wherever local electronics service capacity exists, a manufacturer can test a product pivot in days — prototype the board, flash the firmware, instrument the line — and every solved problem leaves expertise behind. Regional expertise compounds into regional advantage: the place gets faster at change itself, which is the scarcest industrial capability of this decade. Wherever that capacity is absent, every adjustment routes through a queue on another continent, and the feedback loop that drives adaptation never forms.

This is why the first physical move of any serious reindustrialization effort should be an electronics bench, not a campus, not a strategy document, not a grant programme. A bench producing real work, training real people, and making the oxygen locally available. Everything else follows from that — or, without it, nothing does.

## What Must Be Built

My proposal is not a panacea but it does take dead aim at one of the valves pumping life throughout each national economy. Reviving our productive capacities in the very places that pioneered the spirits of mass production and advanced machining is the first but critical step for economic and cultural recovery across the West, for which the term Anglosphere was only ever a proxy, a placeholder and signifier for the extended family of peoples who closely share ideas about the good life and therefore how business should be organized and regulated by the state.

This is not a plan for a single business. This is a protocol for industrial recovery that anyone can implement. It is open, amenable, and overall a conversation about what will work and what can be repeated, so that any group willing to take the lead in their area can act fast and effectively.

The main thesis is straightforward: based on our need to reindustrialize across the Anglosphere, the new centrality of computers and automation systems, and the ongoing logistics challenges faced by small and medium producers and processors of hard goods, we need a background entity whose purpose is to help companies accelerate their daily output and evolution — to lower the output-drag of their region by providing what the ecosystem no longer provides on its own.

The response is a new kind of company — a Platform-Franchise named Clark — that marries the strength of a common brand and operational standards with an open model for spawning regional entities that serve the unique needs of an area while delivering a core set of high-value universal services.

Clark is an Industrial Ecosystem Platform. It delivers its services through four integrated layers, and the integration matters as much as any individual layer, because nobody currently operates all four as a single loop:

The first layer is electronics education and industrial intelligence — certified training in electronics production and repair, supply chain awareness and industrial literacy, IPC-standard credentials delivered at bench level for hundreds of dollars rather than enrolment years, with field reports and registries forming the curriculum's living textbook. This is Marshall's knowledge spillover, made deliberate.

The second layer is electronics processing and assembly — small-batch custom electronics creation, responsive product development, and the provision of local fabrication capacity that eliminates the queue to a distant continent. This is the oxygen supply. One bench in a mid-size city can credibly serve customers on other continents, and it costs a thousandth of the flagship campus that strategy documents defer to the long term.

The third layer is production and logistics software — open-source tools for local operations, facility-aware systems that support role evolution and product-line pivoting without the retooling cost that chains small manufacturers to their past. This is Marshall's subsidiary industries, digitized: shared infrastructure that no single small firm could build alone.

The fourth layer is capital brokering and regional acquisition. This is the mechanism that distinguishes Clark from a training provider or a consultancy. Each Clark node is anchored by and co-owned with a regional ecosystem entity — call it Niagara Assembly, or whatever name the region earns. That anchor entity, operating under the Clark brand and with Clark's counsel and resources, progressively develops the sophistication to perform mergers and acquisitions within its own ecosystem: acquiring key assets, properties, and companies to ensure the ecosystem's powerful operation and acceleration. This is not abstract. It is the model proven by Constellation Software — over eleven hundred permanent-hold acquisitions generating 11.6 billion dollars in annual revenue — and by HEICO, whose subsidiaries are empowered to acquire on their own initiative. It is the keiretsu principle of minority equity, shared methodology, and geographic clustering, adapted for Common Law jurisdictions where contract-heavy, distributed enterprise is structurally natural.

The progression is always physical: observation of a region's ground truth, then a bench producing real work, then a training centre built around that bench, then an assembly-capable node, then a regional acquirer strengthening the ecosystem through deliberate consolidation of its most productive assets. The physical centre is not optional. It is not a nice-to-have aspiration deferred to a mature phase. It is the mechanism itself — the place where knowledge spills over, where the labour pool forms, where the supplier network becomes visible and connected.

## The Starting Ground

Hamilton, Ontario — at the head of the Niagara Peninsula, surrounded by steel, food processing, machinery, and port logistics — is where Clark begins, because this is where the evidence is cleanest and where the pattern is most legible. But Hamilton is not a pilot site in the sequential sense. The urgency of the problem does not permit the luxury of spending five to fifteen years proving a mature model in one place before permitting others to begin. The skilled trades face a five-to-one exit-to-entry ratio; the knowledge holders are retiring faster than they can be replaced; every year of sequential validation is a year of irreversible knowledge loss.

Hamilton and its regional anchor — Niagara Assembly, operating across the peninsula's arc of mid-size industrial towns from St. Catharines to Fort Erie and across the border into Buffalo — will serve as the reference implementation: the experiment that models how to bring an ecosystem activation into existence, documented thoroughly through Clark's own field reports, generating the evidence that other regions study in near-real time. But it is not a gate. Other regions begin their own observation-to-bench progressions as soon as local conditions and partners permit. The methodology travels while the reference implementation is still maturing. This is how Fraunhofer built seventy-six institutes across Germany — not sequentially, but simultaneously, with shared methodology and local adaptation. It is how Constellation Software acquires over a hundred companies per year across geographies without waiting for one to prove the thesis. The protocol is the product, and it is designed to travel.

All five Anglosphere nations are now spending significant sums on reindustrialization — from the trillions authorized under America's CHIPS Act, IRA, and IIJA, to Canada's first Defence Industrial Strategy committing over five hundred billion dollars in a decade, to the UK's Advanced Manufacturing Plan, Australia's National Reconstruction Fund, and New Zealand's industry transformation plans. CANZUK has 72 to 75 per cent public support across its four member nations, representing a combined GDP of over 8.3 trillion dollars. AUKUS is creating actual trilateral industrial cooperation. The Five Eyes framework already treats allied industrial bases as extensions of one another through the National Technology Industrial Base agreement.

The money is flowing. The political will is forming. What is missing is the conversion layer — the entity that stands on the ground in a given region and turns strategy into people building things. Every comparable industrial city I have examined has the same gap: completed plans, approved strategies, allocated budgets, and no mechanism to convert any of it into an ecosystem of producers. Clark is that mechanism — an Industrial Activation Network whose protocol any willing region can adopt. It is the missing entity that every successful ecosystem in history had in some form — the Lit & Phil, the Mechanics' Institute, the Springfield Armory, SAE International, Fred Terman at Stanford — and that every dead ecosystem lost or never built.

The next part of this series will describe the mechanism in detail: how the activation protocol works, what a bench actually produces, how a regional anchor grows from observer to acquirer, and why the economics of a single node demonstrate that this is not philanthropy but a business with defensible margins and compounding returns.
