**Purpose:** Comparative research on models where activation/incubation protocols produce permanent physical centers, and what those centers look like at different maturity stages.

## 1. German Fraunhofer Institutes

### Scale & Structure
- **76 institutes** and independent research units across Germany
- **~32,000 employees**, predominantly qualified scientists and engineers
- **Annual business volume:** EUR 3.6 billion (2024), up 5% year-over-year
- Contract research portion: EUR 3.1 billion (88% of total)

### The Funding Model (The "Fraunhofer Model")
The three-pillar structure:

1. **Base funding (~30%):** Institutional funding from German federal and state governments (90:10 federal/state split). Used for pre-competitive research — solutions that will become relevant to industry within 5-10 years. This is the seed money that gives institutes freedom to take risks.

2. **Industry contracts and license fees (~24%):** EUR 867 million in 2024 (EUR 705M contracts + EUR 162M license fees). 4% increase year-over-year. This is where market validation happens.

3. **Publicly funded competitive projects (~36%):** EUR 1,295 million from federal, state, EU sources. Won through competitive bidding, not guaranteed.

The critical mechanism: **base funding is performance-linked**. An institute that wins more contract revenue earns more base funding. An institute that fails to attract industry contracts sees its base funding shrink. This creates a Darwinian incentive — successful institutes grow, unsuccessful ones contract or close. There are no permanent guaranteed budgets.

### How New Institutes Are Created
- Typically nucleate around a **joint appointment**: an outstanding researcher serves simultaneously as a university professor and Fraunhofer institute director. This dual role links academic research to applied industry work.
- The institute is embedded in a **regional ecosystem** — located near the university and near local industry clusters. Not parachuted in from Berlin.
- 16 "regional high-performance centers" established across 10 German states, where multiple institutes collaborate with local industry on related topics.
- New institutes can be created (or old ones dissolved) based on industry demand and research potential. After German reunification (1990s), Fraunhofer absorbed and restructured institutes in eastern Germany.

### Governance Reform (2024)
In June 2024, Fraunhofer adopted comprehensive statutory reform:
- Senate strengthened as advisory/supervisory body
- President's unilateral policy authority removed; replaced with collective executive board decisions
- Internal governance between executive board, institutes, and committees clarified
- Based on German Public Corporate Governance Code

### Relationship to Local Industry
Fraunhofer is not an advisory service. It **does the work** alongside industry. Key characteristics:
- Joint research projects with specific companies
- Technology transfer through working prototypes and production-ready processes
- License revenue from patents (EUR 162M/year)
- Staff mobility: researchers move between institutes and industry

### What Fraunhofer Produces vs. What It Enables
**Produces:** Applied research, working prototypes, production processes, patents, trained researchers
**Enables:** Technology transfer to SMEs, regional industrial upgrading, workforce pipeline through university linkages

### Honest Assessment
- The performance-linked funding model is genuinely meritocratic but creates institutional risk aversion — institutes may chase safe contract work over breakthrough research
- The model required 75 years to reach current scale; it did not spring into existence overnight
- Attempts to replicate the model elsewhere (UK Catapults, various US programs) have largely underperformed, suggesting the model is deeply embedded in German institutional culture

## 2. NIST Manufacturing Extension Partnership (MEP)

### Scale & Structure
- **51 MEP Centers** across all 50 states + Puerto Rico
- **~1,400 manufacturing advisors** at 450+ service locations
- **Served 33,500-36,000 manufacturers annually** (2022-2024)
- Total network budget: ~USD 437 million (2025)
- Federal contribution: USD 175 million; remainder from state/local + client fees
- **Cost-share model:** Federal pays ≤50%, states/clients pay ≥50%

### What MEP Actually Does
Core services — all advisory/consulting, never production:

1. **Process improvement:** Lean manufacturing, value stream mapping, operational efficiency
2. **Workforce development:** Leadership training, skills assessment (but NOT bench-level assembler training — they train managers, not workers)
3. **Technology adoption:** Helping SMEs adopt new equipment, automation, cybersecurity
4. **Supply chain optimization:** Connecting manufacturers to domestic suppliers
5. **Business strategy:** Growth planning, market diversification, export assistance
6. **Compliance:** Cybersecurity (CMMC), quality systems, regulatory navigation

### What MEP Does NOT Do (Critical for Clark Comparison)
- **Does not produce anything.** MEP is purely advisory. No bench, no assembly, no manufacturing.
- **Does not train assemblers.** Workforce development means management consulting, not hands-on production skills.
- **Does not acquire companies.** No M&A capability, no capital deployment.
- **Does not create physical centers that manufacture.** MEP centers are office/consulting spaces, not production facilities.
- **Does not publish field reports** or generate independent research.
- **Does not build regional industrial ecosystems.** It advises existing manufacturers one at a time.

### Impact Metrics (FY 2024 — Self-Reported)
- $15 billion in new and retained sales
- $5 billion in new client investments
- $2.6 billion in cost savings
- 108,000+ jobs created or retained
- Net Promoter Score: 85.3

### The OIG Problem (September 2024)
The Commerce Department's Office of Inspector General found that **NIST overstated MEP's economic impacts to Congress**:
- Impact metrics are self-reported by MEP clients with inadequate verification
- NIST used overstated numbers to justify program funding
- Despite a 19% rise in federal funding (2022-2023), MEP reported declining economic impact
- The OIG report (OIG-24-037-I) concluded MEP "overstated the role its services played in manufacturing benefits"

### Political Vulnerability (2025-2026)
- April 2025: Trump administration declined to renew contracts for 10 state MEPs (DE, MS, HI, NV, IA, NM, KS, ND, ME, WY)
- FY2026 presidential budget request: **zero funding for MEP**
- Remaining state contracts expire between July 2025 and March 2026
- Congressional response: Both House and Senate appropriations bills provided $175M anyway
- Status as of mid-2026: Program survival uncertain, some states operating without federal payments

### Honest Assessment
MEP is a **consulting network, not an activation protocol**. It advises existing manufacturers on incremental improvement. It does not create new manufacturing capacity, does not train production workers, does not build physical infrastructure, and does not generate intellectual output. Its impact metrics have been challenged by its own inspector general. Its political vulnerability — nearly eliminated by executive action — demonstrates the fragility of a model that depends entirely on government funding without producing tangible, defensible output.

## 3. UK Catapult Centres

### Scale & Structure
- **9 Catapult centres** in the current network (originally 7)
- **High Value Manufacturing (HVM) Catapult:** 7 founding centres, now 17 sites
- ~3,500 employees including 1,700 apprentices
- Established 2011, modeled explicitly on Fraunhofer

### Funding Model
Target: **one-third / one-third / one-third** split:
1. Core government grant (via Innovate UK)
2. Competitively won public R&D funding
3. Industry contract revenue

Reality: The EY independent review found centres remain **"overwhelmingly reliant" on public funding** and have not achieved the target third from industry.

### What They Were Supposed to Do
Bridge the "valley of death" between university research and commercial production. Provide shared facilities where companies (especially SMEs) could access expensive equipment and expertise to develop new manufacturing processes.

### What Actually Happened — The Critique
The Ernst & Young independent review found:

1. **Inconsistent implementation:** "Implementation of the Catapult concept has been inconsistent and could have had significantly greater impact"
2. **Governance failures:** "Governance has not been sufficiently robust" — Innovate UK failed to intervene when centres missed performance targets
3. **Management concerns:** "Limited evidence" that Catapults are well managed
4. **Over-reliance on public funding:** Centres did not develop sufficient industry revenue streams
5. **Tim Crocker (SME Innovation Alliance):** "The problem is not so much a lack of government support, but a lack of understanding behind the support which makes it ineffective"

The HVM Catapult and Satellite Applications Catapult were noted as exceptions — "proactive" in finding new funding sources.

### Top-Down vs. Bottom-Up (Clark Comparison)
The Catapult failure mode is instructive:
- **Government seeded from the top:** Selected technology areas, designated centres, allocated budgets — before demonstrating demand
- **No bench-first validation:** Centres were designed as large-scale shared facilities, not validated through small-scale activation
- **No performance-linked funding mechanism** like Fraunhofer's — centres received funding regardless of industry engagement
- **No organic regional embedding:** Centres placed at existing institutions rather than growing from demonstrated local need

This is the opposite of Clark's model: Clark starts with a bench, proves the work, then scales the physical center. Catapults started with the physical center and hoped the work would follow.

### Recent Performance (2024-2025)
The HVM Catapult 2025 annual report claims:
- 400+ outreach events reaching 100,000+ young people
- Expansion from 7 to 17 sites
- Various industry partnership projects

But hard financial and impact data remain difficult to extract from public reporting.

## 4. Taiwan's ITRI (Industrial Technology Research Institute)

### The Origin Story
- **Founded:** 1973
- **Initial budget:** NT$210 million (~USD 16 million at the time)
- **Starting staff:** 400 employees, only 10 with PhDs
- **Reporting to:** Ministry of Economic Affairs (not Science/Technology) — accountability for economic value, not academic prestige

### The Mechanism That Created a Semiconductor Ecosystem

**Phase 1 — Technology Acquisition:**
1974: ITRI licensed chip manufacturing technology from RCA (US), paying ~$10M. The government directed this — it was a national strategy, not a market-driven decision.

**Phase 2 — Talent Pipeline:**
- Leveraged Taiwan's mandatory military service: university graduates could fulfill service obligations through 4-year ITRI employment (brilliant mechanism — conscription as workforce development)
- Recruited 400 top engineering graduates annually from Taiwan's best universities
- Sponsored employees for advanced degrees at Stanford and MIT (~$400K per employee)
- Grew PhD workforce from 10 to 900 among 6,000 employees by late 1990s
- **Critical design choice:** Targeted 15% annual staff turnover — deliberately pushed trained people into private industry

**Phase 3 — Spinoff Creation:**
The spinoff mechanism was direct and tangible:
- ITRI transferred technology, equipment, and trained personnel to new companies
- Provided demonstration facilities and manufacturing plants
- Recruited incumbent firms as capital investors (aligning stakeholder interests)
- The government did NOT retain permanent ownership — ventures were pushed toward market validation

**Phase 4 — Ecosystem Infrastructure:**
1979: Hsinchu Science-Based Industrial Park established with tax benefits, low-interest loans, tariff exemptions. By late 1990s, ~40% of park managers had prior ITRI experience. 85% of departing ITRI staff joined Taiwanese firms.

### Major Spinoffs
| Company | Year | Current Status |
|---------|------|----------------|
| United Microelectronics (UMC) | 1980 | ~$20B valuation |
| TSMC | 1987 | World's most valuable semiconductor company |
| Taiwan Mask Corporation | 1988 | Operating |
| Vanguard International Semiconductor | 1994 | Operating |
| ~160 total formal spinoffs | 1973-present | Plus hundreds of informal spawns |

### TSMC Specifically
ITRI's president Morris Chang recognized Taiwan should "lean into its manufacturing strengths and serve as a dedicated chip manufacturer." The pure-play foundry model was born. Philips (Netherlands) provided capital and existing technology; ITRI contributed trained workforce and accumulated know-how. 98 professionals transferred from ITRI to TSMC at founding.

### Budget & Scale
- Current budget: ~$600M/year (as of most recent available data)
- Revenue: ~$450M
- Employees: 5,000-10,000 (sources vary)
- 162 formal spinoff companies total

### Can This Work at Regional Scale?
The honest answer: **ITRI was a national-scale intervention backed by sovereign industrial policy.** The budget ($600M/year), the conscription-as-workforce-pipeline trick, the ability to direct technology licensing from foreign companies — these are national government tools.

However, the **mechanism** is instructive at any scale:
1. Acquire capability (technology + people)
2. Train intensively in a working facility
3. Spin off trained people into new ventures
4. Build physical infrastructure (science parks) to house the ecosystem
5. Accept that being supplanted by your spinoffs is success, not failure

ITRI's budget was actually **cancelled in 1993** when established semiconductor firms argued the institute created unfair competition. As the Asterisk article noted: "being supplanted by its children is the ideal outcome for an applied research institute."

## 5. Fab Labs / Makerspaces That Became Production Facilities

### The Structural Problem
The fab lab/makerspace movement has a **policy-level barrier** to production transition. Per the Fab Foundation itself: "Commercial activities can be prototyped and incubated in a fab lab, but they must not conflict with other uses, they should grow beyond rather than within the lab." The design philosophy explicitly pushes commercial operations OUT of the lab rather than allowing them to mature in place.

### The Fab City Movement
The most ambitious attempt to bridge this gap:
- Launched by IAAC (Barcelona), MIT's CBA, Barcelona City Council, and the Fab Foundation
- Goal: shift cities from PITO (Products In, Trash Out) to DIDO (Data In, Data Out) — local production from shared digital designs
- Pledges urban self-sufficiency by 2054
- ~1,000 fab labs in 78+ countries
- Projects include solar/wind turbines, custom housing

But Fab City remains aspirational. No fab lab has become a significant production facility.

### Documented Transitions (Makerspace → Commercial)
Specific examples where prototyping in makerspaces launched companies:

| Company | Origin | Outcome |
|---------|--------|---------|
| **Pebble Smartwatch** | Prototyped at TechShop | Kickstarter record, shipped product, later acquired by Fitbit |
| **Square** | Early prototype built at makerspace | $10M VC funding → $50B+ company |
| **Instavan** | Started at Urban Workshop (Costa Mesa, CA) | Commercial auto-upfitting business |

### What Usually Prevents the Transition
1. **Institutional design:** Fab labs are designed as shared community resources, not production facilities. Commercial use conflicts with open-access ethos.
2. **Equipment mismatch:** Fab lab tools (laser cutters, 3D printers, CNC mills) are prototyping tools, not production equipment. The gap between "making one" and "making one thousand" requires entirely different capital equipment.
3. **No business infrastructure:** Fab labs provide tools, not business formation support, capital access, supply chain relationships, or quality management systems.
4. **Culture gap:** Maker culture values exploration and sharing; production culture values repeatability and efficiency. These are genuinely different orientations.
5. **Space constraints:** Fab labs are typically 100-300 sqm. Production needs 1,000+ sqm minimum.

### Key Insight for Clark
The makerspace movement proves that **access to tools creates activity** but does NOT automatically create production capacity. Clark's model is different because:
- The bench IS a production station, not a prototyping station
- Training IS for production work (assembly), not exploration
- The protocol includes business formation, not just tool access
- The physical center IS a production facility, not a community workshop

## 6. Meanwhile Space / Temporary Activation → Permanent Tenancy

### Meanwhile Space CIC (London, est. 2009)
- Market leader in "meanwhile use" — placing temporary tenants in vacant commercial properties
- Social enterprise (Community Interest Company)
- Model: negotiate temporary access to vacant buildings, place creative/community tenants, demonstrate viability, enable transition to permanent commercial tenancy

**Key example — Central Parade:** Transformed a Grade II listed building into a creative hub for 20 businesses (retail, freelancers, cafe). Originally a 2-year meanwhile use, **extended to 5 years** before operation transferred to local council. This is the activation-to-permanence arc.

**Limitation:** Meanwhile Space does not publish systematic conversion-rate data. The model is designed to make the space attractive enough that property owners can find commercial tenants — but Meanwhile Space itself does not track what percentage of temporary tenants become permanent ones.

### Renew Newcastle (Australia, est. 2009)
Marcus Westbury's model for revitalizing Newcastle, NSW:

- **200+ proposals received**, 70+ creative projects facilitated in empty spaces
- **100+ vacant properties reopened** across Australia (via Renew Australia expansion)
- **30-day rolling license model** — minimizes risk for both property owners and tenants
- Described as "a permanent structure for temporary things" — the organization is permanent, the individual activations are temporary

**Results:**
- "Scores of jobs created, hundreds of businesses and community projects started"
- Most Renew projects on Hunter Street Mall ended after bringing back foot traffic — enabling property owners to attract commercial tenants
- Some incubated projects became permanent, viable businesses paying commercial rent
- Westbury reports 60+ individual space activations

**The conversion mechanism:** Renew Newcastle does not convert temporary tenants to permanent tenants directly. It uses temporary activation to **prove the location is viable**, which enables property owners to attract entirely new permanent tenants. The temporary tenants are the proof-of-concept; the permanent tenants are different entities.

### Spaceworks Tacoma (USA, est. ~2010)
Joint initiative of City of Tacoma and Tacoma-Pierce County Chamber:

- **100+ businesses incubated** through training and space access
- **40+ new businesses launched** in Tacoma
- **6-month rent-free period**, then transition to market-rate rent
- Serves 130+ microenterprises annually through training cohorts, technical assistance, microloans
- "Creative Enterprise" program matches business owners with landlords willing to negotiate reduced rent

**Conversion evidence:** Tenants "often express interest in staying beyond their Spaceworks term as paying tenants." But no systematic conversion-rate data published.

### Baltimore Bromo Arts District
- 117-acre arts district established 2012
- **BOOST Program** (Black Owned and Operated Storefront Tenancy): $10M/year for 3 years from state
- Provides grants, education, lease assistance, wraparound support
- Five initial merchants selected in 2021 for brick-and-mortar locations
- Strategic storefront placement designed to create community between tenants

### Aggregate Assessment
**What we know:**
- Temporary activation programs reliably reduce vacancy, increase foot traffic, and demonstrate location viability
- They create real businesses and jobs
- Some temporary tenants become permanent, but the more common pattern is that temporary activation enables DIFFERENT permanent tenants

**What we don't know:**
- Systematic conversion rates (no program publishes this data rigorously)
- Long-term survival rates of businesses incubated through these programs
- Whether the activated areas remain vital after the program withdraws

**Key difference from Clark:** These programs activate EXISTING buildings with EXISTING tenants doing VARIOUS things. Clark activates a SPECIFIC capability (electronics assembly) and builds a PERMANENT center around it. The meanwhile model is about place; Clark is about production.

## 7. German Lernfabriken (Learning Factories)

### TU Darmstadt — Center for Industrial Production (CiP)
- **Founded:** 2007, on Lichtwiese Campus
- **Focus:** Lean Production and Industry 4.0 competency development
- **Pedagogical model:** Production environment representative of real SMEs. Existing devices and processes optimized from a **previous (imperfect) state** rather than created in ideal form — trainees learn improvement, not greenfield design.
- Key insight: The learning factory deliberately starts in a degraded state so trainees can practice the act of improvement. This is pedagogically intentional.

### TU Darmstadt — ETA-Fabrik (Energy Efficiency)
- **Opened:** 2016 (construction began 2013)
- **Funded by:** BMWi (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs), State of Hesse, TU Darmstadt
- **Purpose:** Holistic energy management research and training
- Real-time data collection from production line and infrastructure
- Serves as test field for industry-academic energy efficiency research

### Festo Learning Factory, Scharnhausen
- **Operated by:** Festo AG since 2014
- **Area:** 220 sqm, divided into four sections: machining, assembly, multi-disciplinary skills, process optimization + media center
- **Critical feature:** The learning factory is **embedded inside the actual production facility** (the Festo Technology Factory in Scharnhausen). Real valves, valve clusters, and electronics are produced right next to the learning stations.
- This is NOT a simulation — it is training adjacent to and interleaved with real production

### The Broader German Learning Factory Landscape
- Initiative "European Learning Factories" founded 2011 at first Conference on Learning Factories in Darmstadt
- Most German learning factories concentrated in southern Germany, where industrial density is highest
- Concept originated in late 1980s with CIM (Computer Integrated Manufacturing) qualification programs
- Universities and companies operate them; they serve industry employees, students, and researchers

### Have Any Spawned Production Operations?
**The Festo Scharnhausen model is the closest to Clark's vision** — it is simultaneously a learning facility AND a production facility. But Festo built the factory first and embedded learning second. The learning factory did not spawn the production operation; the production operation absorbed a learning function.

No documented cases found of a standalone German learning factory spawning an independent commercial production operation. Learning factories remain pedagogical — they teach people who then go work in existing factories.

### Cost Structure
- CiP and similar university learning factories operate on university + government research funding
- Corporate learning factories (Festo) are funded as corporate training expenses
- Typical investment: EUR 1-5M for a university learning factory; corporate facilities vary widely
- Operating costs embedded in either university budgets or corporate training budgets

## 8. Singapore: A*STAR and JTC Industrial Estates

### JTC Corporation — The Physical Infrastructure Builder
- **Founded:** 1968 (three years after independence)
- **Status:** Statutory board under Ministry of Trade and Industry
- **Portfolio:** 6,800-7,000 hectares of industrial land — **over 80% of all industrial land in Singapore**
- **Annual revenue:** SGD ~1.5B (USD ~1.1B as of 2026)
- **Model:** Government builds and owns industrial infrastructure; private companies are tenants

Key estates:
- **Jurong Island:** Petrochemical complex (100+ companies on a single island)
- **One-North:** R&D and biomedical hub (houses Biopolis, Fusionopolis)
- **Jurong Innovation District:** Advanced manufacturing + A*STAR research institutes
- **Punggol Digital District:** Tech and digital industries

**The JTC mechanism:** JTC acquires land (Singapore's government owns ~90% of all land), builds purpose-designed industrial facilities (flatted factories, specialized labs, cleanrooms), and leases them to private operators. Rental revenue flows back to government for reinvestment. This is not a market-driven process — it is **deliberate state construction of industrial capacity** that private operators then fill.

### A*STAR — The Research-to-Industry Bridge
- **Agency for Science, Technology and Research**
- **Part of:** Singapore's RIE (Research, Innovation, Enterprise) framework
- **RIE2025 budget:** SGD 25 billion (increased by additional SGD 3B)
- **Spinoffs:** 70+ new companies over the past 5 years; notable exits include Mirxes, Lucence, Respiree, Nuevocor
- **Key research institutes:** SIMTech (manufacturing), NMC (nanomaterials), EDDC (drug development), DxD Hub (diagnostics)

**Translational platforms:**
- A*STAR AI Centre of Excellence in Manufacturing (AIMfg): supported ~30 firms since 2024 launch
- National Semiconductor Translation and Innovation Centre (NSTIC): SGD 180M budget
- Nucleic Acid Therapeutics Initiative (NATi): SGD 97M
- MedTech Catapult: SGD 38M

### The Combined JTC + A*STAR Model
The distinctive feature of Singapore's approach:
1. **JTC builds the physical space** (industrial estates, purpose-built facilities)
2. **A*STAR provides the research capability** (institutes co-located in JTC facilities)
3. **Private companies are tenants** in government-built infrastructure alongside government research
4. **The state creates the conditions;** the private sector fills the productive capacity

Example: A*STAR's SIMTech and NMC research institutes were anchored at Jurong Innovation District specifically to "push the boundaries of smarter, greener and more connected manufacturing" for co-located private manufacturers.

### Relevance to Clark
Singapore's model validates the principle that **building the physical infrastructure first creates the conditions for private productive activity.** But it operates at sovereign scale:
- Government owns 90% of land
- Multi-billion-dollar budgets
- Decades-long planning horizons
- Population of 5.9M in a city-state with total control over land use

Clark's advantage: it doesn't need sovereign land control or billion-dollar budgets. It starts with a single bench ($10-25K) and grows the center organically. But the Singapore model shows that the principle — build the facility, populate with capability, enable private production — works at scale.

### Honest Assessment
- Singapore's model requires an authoritarian (or at minimum, highly centralized) governance structure
- JTC's rental revenue model means the government profits from the industrial ecosystem it creates — sustainability through revenue, not ongoing subsidy
- A*STAR's spinoff track record (70+ companies in 5 years) is real but modest compared to ITRI's semiconductor transformation
- The model is essentially impossible to replicate in a federated system like the US or in countries without state land ownership
